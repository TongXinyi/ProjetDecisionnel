  1. Introduction

Our project focuses on the study of music popularity mainly based on their objective attributes like their duration and their lyrics. We aim to answer broad questions about general music, artists, and album statistics as well as more specific questions about word occurence in lyrics for instance. In order to do this, we mainly rely on open music data sources like Pitchfork reviews and Spotify.

In this second phase, we used an iterative approach called requirement-based design to model our data warehouse. This method is based on the requirements to iteratively refine the facts in relation to the available data sources. This will lead us to design our extraction, transformation and loading process of the data sources which will then be loaded into a concrete data warehouse database.

All of the documentation, schemas and descriptions for is project are kept updated on this website:

https://bencrulis.github.io/BDMA_M1_Project/

  1. Workload

We recall the typical workload our data warehouse will answer:

  • What’s the maximal, minimal, and average number of songs by artist, genre, years and album? We want to know if the most successful music type is associated with the productivity of the artist and the prevalence of the genre.
  • What’s the maximal, minimal, and average reviews score by artist, genre, year, and album? We want to know the most popular artist, the most popular album, and the most popular music genre.
  • What’s the maximal, minimal, and average number of words of the different featured songs? By analyzing this, we can understand how the number of words influence a song popularity.
  • Does the pareto principle apply to the data? We want to understand if a small portion of the artist is responsible for the creation of the majority of songs.
  • What are the most common words by song, genre, country and year? This will help us understand if some words are associated with popular genre.
  • What’s the top genre, artists and albums by country and years? From this question we can understand if in different country and in different years songs have different popularity standards.
  • Which genre and tags of music have more reviews than others? By analyzing this, we can find which genre attracts most criticism.
  • How are song influenced by their proportion of stopwords in their lyrics? We try to find out if lyrics complexity is correlated with their success.
  • Does the Zipf’s law apply to the data? This question will help us to understand if being already successful helps increasing popularity further.

  1. Facts schema and aggregation matrices

We chose two facts as the basis for our data exploration. We emphasize the difference between a song, which contains lyrics, with musics, which isn't necessarily associated to some lyrics.

For each fact, we describe the event it represents, provide a schema with its dimensions and measures.

We also describe the aggregation matrix which shows how roll-up operations can affect measure aggregation. We then finally represent how each fact attribute is mapped to a database table attribute.

We propose three main facts for our study, Music Release, Music Review and finally Music Ranking.

  • The first fact represent the publication of a song.

  • The second fact represents a review given for a music by a reviewer on a given date.

  • The third fact represents a rank based on view count given to a music on a given date in a given country. Streams represents the total number of times the music was streamed at this date.
